Il Miglior Fabbro

Words by Ryan Bowman

Edited by Alice Brett & Jon Steinback

For most of history, editors worked only with dead authors. These first text workers, or copy-readers as they came to be called in the Victorian era, were the scribes of antiquity – paid to literally rewrite texts (to which they only rarely, and daringly, added their own slight, amanuensis-style adaptations).

As soon as we got living, breathing authors though, in the early 16th-century, we got editors to match. The spread of the printing press created the first glut of text and text creators, which in turn spurred the need for cultural and linguistic arbiters. Editors, anonymous today, in this nascent world were gods (or at least men of God: many were friars). In fact, in Venice and Florence, then publishing capitals of the world, readers sought out the work of editors, not authors. Imagine being excited for the latest Sonny Mehta instead of the latest Ishiguro. Do you know who edited the last book you read?

The Editor's Work

Editors aren't just living literary technology or interpretative machines, though they are both. They work primarily in the interest of the communicative goal of the author and the social context in which that goal is to be received. In other words, they work with people and ideas.

In his notebooks, Henry James claimed to be able to "see" the end state of his writing, the ideal form it already had if he could only find it. It is an editor's job to share this vision with the writer – not to overlay their own interpretation over the original and in the process blur the picture.

Maxwell Perkins, the archetypal "editor of genius" who shepherded Fitzgerald and Hemingway's chaotic careers, is famed for responding to desperate writer letters with an offer of an evening at the pub. Frantic authors would arrive with sheafs of paper, ready to dig in, only to find Perkins sitting in comfortable silence with a newspaper and two drinks. A genial evening of light inebriation and conversation would follow, at the end of which the author would return home, all their problems solved.

Which isn't to say editing is all about barstool psychology. After all, Perkins also cut 90,000 words from Thomas Wolfe's Look Homeward, Angel. But the best editors work with their shared vision as a map; transforming editorial work into archaeological uncovering (in the Stephen King tradition).

Take literary impresario Ezra Pound and his almost-mythic role in editing Eliot's The Waste Land as a prime example (unfortunately, this essay does not deal with his also-almost-mythic throwing of Robert Frost across a restaurant during his jujitsu phase; for another time). Pound, whom Eliot dubbed il miglior fabbro, the better craftsman, in his dedication for The Waste Land, viciously cut half of the poem in a single 1921 session. But Eliot later said, "He was a marvellous critic because he didn’t try to turn you into an imitation of himself. He tried to see what you were trying to do."

More Than You Are Capable Of

Editors live unenviable lives. It is an empathetic art they practise; the form they take is cast not of themselves but in the likeness of the writer - a labor of the shadows, as Knausgaard puts it. And yet, with an editor, writing can soar. Without, it can plummet.

I will leave you with Fitzgerald, who describes in a letter to Perkins what he means to him and what we want Marker to mean to you. Speaking in the midst of the The Great Gatsby, then known by its terrible initial name "Trimalchio in West Egg", he writes to Perkins, "I cannot let it go out unless it has the very best I'm capable of in it or even as I feel sometimes, something better than I'm capable of."
