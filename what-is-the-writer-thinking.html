What is the
writer thinking?

Working out what we think is the most ordinary thing we do. It's the least supported.

Words by Jon Steinback
Edited by Katy Zack & Ryan Bowman

"What was John Steinbeck thinking?"

Ms. Britton, my perfectly-named eighth grade English teacher, surveyed our class, waiting for a raised hand to materialise. We were one week into our month of Of Mice and Men. In my memory, she asked this question every day as we progressed through the book, evoking in me a constant sense of teenage panic due to our confusingly similar names. "What was Steinbeck thinking?"

At 13, I was near the start of my personal writing journey. I hadn't yet written for the school paper, nor entered (and lost) any literary contests. I'd yet to study writing in college, and certainly couldn't have imagined a future career centred around the written word and communicating ideas.

A decade later, I was teaching English myself and slowly working the class through Italo Calvino's Invisible Cities. The book is a disorienting amalgam of abstract symbols and liminal realities, and seems designed to impact each reader differently, and each student expressed different reactions. As a class, we discussed Calvino's intent, and how it made each student feel.

"What was Calvino thinking?"

Our guiding principle when conceiving of Marker was Joan Didion's famous line, "I write entirely to find out what I'm thinking." How do you map your thoughts to words? How does your vision change as you reflect? Whether you're writing a personal email or a novel, you're going through this process.

But we've been abandoned in the wild without tools to help us do that.

When you open up a blank document in your existing 'word processor,' you're greeted by dozens of type-setting options, but not a single feature that helps you write. The only head-nod towards actual writing is the numb persistence of jagged blue grammatical suggestions.

You might, instead, seek help from a large language model (LLM), which has the 'goal' of efficient production of text (which they do prodigiously). Even if you overlook the cliches and em dashes and repeated structures, you feel the growing disconnect between ideas in your head, and the nuance and meaning introduced by each word choice that is not your own.

It's remarkable that neither word processors or LLMs (the tools billions of people use) actually help you make sense of what you're thinking.

With Marker we want to chart a better path. A tool that helps with actual writing. A writer-first environment that helps a person stay in that place of discovery, of thinking. An editor that reflects your writing back to you, to elucidate and challenge and reveal. A platform that helps your collaborators be better helpers, and helps you make the most of the feedback.

If you're a professional author, like a novelist or journalist, you often have access to people who help you in each part of the writing process. Your journey from thoughts to words is aided by experts illuminating the path. But most people don't.

Writing, the adage goes, is a group activity you do alone. Why can't everyone, writing anything, have access to that support? How can writing, the most universal of human creative endeavours, be the most underserved by our creative tools?

We initially codenamed Marker 'Acknowledgements' (we both love reading these at the end of a book or essay), because the acknowledgements show the breadth of that help and support an author relies upon to hone their work. The very first reader, the expert that helped fill in some blanks, the detail-oriented editor, or often just the encouraging friend. Each acknowledgement played a crucial role in that intentful, thinking journey of discovery.

We've spoken to hundreds of writers while building Marker. Many came in frustrated with the piece they'd been working on, knowing what they wanted to say but not sure they'd said it (a nine-time novelist told us she still feels crippling anxiety before sharing each first draft!). And using Marker, they keep telling us how Marker pushed their thinking… how it helped them figure out what they were trying to say.

"What are you thinking?"

There is a magic feeling when your ideas take shape on the page, vividly expressed. It's why we write. If we build Marker right, everyone can experience more of that. And feel, with conviction, that the words on the page communicate exactly what they are thinking.
